What it is

Care cards highlight when a user should take steps to contact the health and care system. There are three different styles, depending on the level of urgency the situation requires.

User need

As someone with symptoms or a condition, I NEED to know if I should contact someone about my health, SO THAT I don’t leave it too late to get the right care.

As someone with symptoms or a condition, I NEED to understand how urgently to contact someone, SO THAT I am treated at the right time.

As someone with symptoms or a condition, I NEED to understand who to contact, SO THAT I do not waste time contacting the wrong service.

NHS need

As an NHS content designer, I NEED to draw a user’s attention to specific actions, SO THAT they take steps to get the right care.

As an NHS content designer, I NEED to be able to communicate the level of urgency an action has, both visually and with text, SO THAT users who are skim-reading, or using screen readers, know how important this information is.

Usage

You can use multiple care cards on a single page, when for example, different levels of severity of a condition require people to access different levels of care. It is up to the content designer to decide where in the content each care card should be placed, and in what order (there are no rules on which should come first).

Only use care cards when directing people to engage directly with the health and care system. They should not be used when directing users to apply online for services (e.g. self-referrals for therapy services, or ordering chlamydia test kits). They should not be used to direct users to contact other parties (e.g. The Samaritans, or charities). You should use info boxes or inset text components to do this.

Non-urgent care card

When to use

Use a non-urgent care card when advising people to contact a healthcare professional about their conditions or symptoms, when it is not time-sensitive. For example, booking a GP appointment, or getting advice from a pharmacist.

Urgent Care Card

When to use

Use an urgent care card when advising people to contact a healthcare professional about their conditions or symptoms, when it is time sensitive. For example, if people are experiencing an allergic reaction to medication, or are in a situation where getting advice or care quickly, is important. An urgent care card can be used to advise people to contact 111, booking an urgent GP appointment, or going to A&E.

Where it's currently being used

Immediate Care Card

When to use

Use an immediate care card when advising people to dial 999, or go immediately to a hospital. This care card should be used only when conditions or symptoms are considered an emergency.

Accessibility

Screen reader users may be unable to distinguish the urgency message of care cards via their colour. Therefore text that is read by screen readers but hidden visually can be added to the header of the care card to indicate its urgency.

This also aids those who may use headings to navigate the page as this text will be part of the heading.

<h3><span class="visually-hidden">Non urgent care: </span>Speak to a GP if</h3>

For those who use high contrast mode in Windows, the colours of the care cards are indistinct so the level of urgency is not conveyed.

To try and give some level of importance to the care cards, a border is shown in this mode to help distinguish care cards from the rest of the content.

Research

The overall design of care cards has tested well in numerous labs, amongst broader content testing. Some accessibility testing raised issues around hidden text used to communicate importance. This has been resolved. See the Accessibility section for more details.

Design decisions

The design of care cards is deliberately distinct from other callout boxes and components, to ensure their message is clear. The full-width, coloured block at the top of each card is meant to make the user pause, if scrolling/scanning down a page.

NHS blue is used for non-urgent care, to be reassuring, whereas red is used for the urgent and immediate cards, to draw the eye and communicate their importance. Small downward arrows are used to guide the eye straight to the content in the card. The immediate care card uses a dark grey background to stand out above all other page content, without being too alarming. Research had shown that a previous iteration that was completely red, was too worrying for some users.
